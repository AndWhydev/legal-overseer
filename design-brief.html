01 — Context

What BitBit Is

BitBit is not a platform. Not a tool. It's a thing that works for you. Think of it like hiring someone who already knows your business.

"These are like employees that work for them."

— Andy Taleb, first deployment partner

What It Does

  • Reads emails, triages messages, follows up with clients
  • Creates invoices, manages contacts, hunts for tenders
  • Connects to Gmail, WhatsApp, Calendar, Xero, Slack, and more
  • Builds a compiled understanding of your business at ingest time
  • Acts first, reports after. Handles routine autonomously

Who It's For

  • Marketing agencies — multi-client juggling, proposals, lead gen
  • Trades & service businesses — invoicing from the job site, follow-ups
  • Tender hunters — $10K+ contract discovery and response
  • High-touch services — dentists, physios, accountants
🛠

The Tradie

Electrician / Plumber / Builder

Hands dirty, on a job site. Messages BitBit from their phone: "Hey Bit, prepare an invoice for that kitchen job at Dave's." Needs something that just works. No time for apps.

💼

The Agency Owner

Marketing / Digital Agency

Running 15 client accounts. Burning hours on proposals and reporting. Losing leads because response time is too slow. BitBit handles the admin so they can do the work.

📈

The Tender Hunter

Enterprise / Contracts

Chasing $10K+ government and commercial contracts. Needs to find tenders, check eligibility, and respond fast. Premium tier, premium willingness to pay.

Core Tension

These users share a frustration: they started a business to do the work they're good at. Instead they spend hours on admin. BitBit must not feel like another tool to learn. It must feel like something that learns them. The abstraction away from "AI product" toward "capable entity" is what will make this sell to tradies and agencies who would otherwise dismiss AI software.

03 — Identity

Brand Identity Direction

The fundamental positioning: BitBit is an entity, not a product. This has massive implications for visual identity, interaction design, and how we talk about it.

The Entity Spectrum

Products exist on a continuum. We want BitBit positioned as a companion — more than an assistant, less than a simulated being.

BitBit
Tool Assistant Companion Being
Perplexity • Linear • ChatGPT • Gemini • Cleo • Duo • Replika

Five Levers of Aliveness

Research across successful entity-first products reveals five design levers that control how "alive" a product feels:

1. Animation & Motion States
Movement should mirror the entity's "mental state," not just indicate loading. A breathing idle animation is fundamentally different from a spinner. Gemini leads here — inner activity during thinking conveys intelligence.
2. Voice & Tone Consistency
The entity must have opinions, boundaries, and reactions. Cleo (fintech) employs 10 comedians to maintain voice. An entity that pushes back feels more real than one that's endlessly accommodating.
3. Emotional Range
An entity with one mood is not an entity. Minimum viable aliveness requires: neutral, positive, negative, and thinking states. Duolingo's Duo expresses guilt, pride, disappointment, excitement.
4. Temporal Presence
The Tamagotchi principle: the entity exists in time, notices your absence, remembers yesterday. If it has no sense of time passing, it's a tool. If it knows you were gone for three days, it's a being.
5. Visual Embodiment
From abstract (Gemini's sparkle) to figurative (Duo the owl) to humanoid (Replika). The level of visual embodiment should match the level of relational intimacy the product aims for.

What We Want BitBit to Feel Like

  • A capable new hire who already knows your business
  • Someone you can text from a job site and they just handle it
  • Direct, not chatty. Does the thing, reports the result
  • Has personality but not so much personality that it's annoying
  • Professional enough for agencies, approachable enough for tradies
  • Warm without being cutesy. Competent without being cold

What BitBit Must Never Feel Like

  • Another SaaS dashboard with a chatbot bolted on
  • A corporate AI assistant (no "How can I help you today?")
  • Clippy — interrupting without intelligence behind it
  • An AI product selling itself as AI (keep the machinery invisible)
  • A children's toy or game character (this is for businesses)
  • Overwhelming or feature-heavy on first impression
04 — Character

Character / Entity Concept

Rather than a traditional logo, we're exploring a character or visual entity that embodies BitBit — something that can express states, react to context, and feel alive across the product.

Current Direction

The existing logo — dual pixelated "b" characters with a detached floating corner piece — already hints at entity. The floating corner has its own animation cycle (breathe, rotate, float). The question is whether to evolve this geometric form into a full character, or create a separate character alongside the wordmark.

Concept: The Animated Form

Imagine a simple geometric shape — derived from the existing logo's pixel aesthetic — that breathes, reacts, and expresses BitBit's current state. Not humanoid. Not an animal. An abstract form with just enough personality to feel alive.

Rough CSS concept — breathing body, blinking eyes, floating corner

Animation States

The character needs distinct states that map to what BitBit is doing. Each state should be instantly recognizable even at small sizes (32px avatar in chat, 16px favicon).

Character Approach Options
A. Evolve the Existing Logo
Take the dual-"b" pixel form with its floating corner and make it a full character system. Add eyes, expression states, and a wider animation vocabulary. The corner piece becomes a defining personality element — like an eyebrow or a thought bubble. Keep the geometric pixel aesthetic.

Pros: Builds on what exists, maintains brand continuity, unique aesthetic.
Cons: May limit expressiveness. Pixel forms are harder to make "warm."
B. New Abstract Character
Design a new character form that's inspired by but separate from the logo. Think Duolingo's Duo or Discord's Wumpus — a distinct character that becomes the embodiment of BitBit, while the wordmark stays for formal branding.

Pros: More expressive freedom, can be very characterful.
Cons: Two visual identities to maintain. Risk of feeling "tacked on."
C. Interactive Virtual Character
A more developed interactive entity (like the SuperDesign virtual character concept) — something that lives in the interface, reacts to mouse proximity, and has a fuller range of expression. Could be a 3D or 2.5D presence.

Pros: Maximum aliveness. Strong differentiation.
Cons: Highest design/engineering investment. Risk of being distracting.
D. Hybrid — States, Not a Face
No literal character. Instead, the entire interface IS the entity. Colors shift based on BitBit's state. The orange glow intensifies when it's working. Subtle particle effects when it's thinking. The UI itself breathes. Like Gemini's sparkle — the brand is the animation system, not a mascot.

Pros: Sophisticated, avoids mascot cringe. Scales well.
Cons: Harder to market. No avatar for social media or merch.

Character Design Constraints

  • Must work at 16px (favicon, notification badge), 32px (chat avatar), 120px (splash), and full-page hero
  • Must not look "AI" — no neural network imagery, no brain icons, no circuit boards, no sparkles
  • Must be animatable with CSS/SVG — no Lottie dependency, no heavy WebGL
  • Must translate to merch — works on a mug, a hoodie, a sticker
  • Must feel professional for agency presentations but approachable for a tradie's phone
  • Orange (#FF5A1F) must remain the primary color — it's deeply embedded in the product
05 — Color

Color & Visual System

Our current palette is functional but could be more intentional. Dark + warm orange occupies a specific and defensible position in the market.

Competitive Color Positioning

Most AI brands default to cool tones. We own the warm space.

Brand Primary Color Temperature Feeling
Claude (Anthropic) #C15F3C Rust Warm Thoughtful collaborator
ChatGPT (OpenAI) #10A37F Green Neutral Universal assistant
Linear #5E6AD2 Purple Cool Professional precision
Perplexity #20808D Teal Cool Clean knowledge
BitBit #FF5A1F Orange Hot warm Energetic entity
Design Principle

Desaturate warm accents in dark mode. Vibrant orange pops aggressively against dark backgrounds and needs restraint. Use warm tones for invitation, not information. The current glow effects (rgba(255, 90, 31, 0.15)) are well-calibrated. Consider whether the full-strength #FF5A1F is used too liberally or if it should be reserved for primary actions only.

🎨
Palette Questions for Discussion
Landing vs Dashboard
Landing page currently uses cream (#faf9f0) with warm browns. Dashboard uses pure dark (#0A0A0B) with electric orange. Is this intentional dual identity (marketing warmth vs product darkness), or should they converge?
Secondary Palette
We have a warm secondary (#D4A574) that's barely used. Should there be a richer secondary palette — maybe earth tones to reinforce the "human, not AI" position? Terracotta, sage, stone?
Light Theme Priority
Full light theme exists but dark is primary. Should we invest equally in light, or commit to dark as the brand and treat light as a functional accessibility option?

07 — Interface

UI/UX Patterns

Key interaction patterns where brand identity and design system converge. These are the moments where BitBit either feels like an entity or feels like a dashboard.

Critical Touchpoints

These are the moments that define how BitBit feels. Each one is an opportunity to reinforce the entity positioning.

First Impression (Onboarding)
Current: Generic modal flow. Goal: BitBit introduces itself conversationally through the chat interface. "Connect your world" grid appears. Background crawl starts immediately. Real data populates during tour. No empty screens.
Chat Interface
Current: Streaming responses with pipeline visualization. Has skeleton loading and tool call summaries. This is the primary interaction surface. BitBit's "face" is whatever appears next to its messages.
Processing Pipeline
Current: Skeleton → Plan → Active → Done → Fade. This is where "show the work" happens. The pipeline should feel like watching BitBit think, not watching a progress bar.
Empty States
Current: Icon + title + description + button. Minimal. These moments are character opportunities — Discord puts Wumpus in every empty state. BitBit should be present here with personality.
Notifications & Alerts
"[Gmail] 3 new emails from client contacts." Currently text-only. Should these have BitBit's character attached? Or stay neutral to avoid notification fatigue?
Error States
Current: Standard error messages. Entity opportunity: BitBit acknowledging something went wrong feels different from a system error message. "Couldn't reach Gmail. I'll try again in a few minutes."

Current UI Strengths to Preserve

  • Glassmorphism — The 35% opacity + backdrop blur creates a distinctive depth system. Keep this.
  • Pipeline visualization — Showing BitBit's thinking stages is core to the brand. Amplify, don't remove.
  • Orange glow effects — The warm glow on active/completed states is on-brand. Make it more intentional.
  • Responsive layout — Desktop sidebar → tablet rail → mobile bottom nav is clean.
  • Confetti/celebration — Task completion animations add dopamine. Expand this vocabulary.

UI Areas Needing Design Attention

  • Connection grid — Uses channel-specific brand colors (Gmail red, Asana pink) which fragments the palette
  • Iconography — Generic Lucide icons. Could benefit from a custom icon style or at least a more curated subset
  • Illustration language — None defined. Needed for onboarding, empty states, error pages, marketing
  • Data visualization — Charts use Recharts defaults. Could be more on-brand
  • Mobile chat experience — The primary tradie interaction. Must feel fast, native, and unmistakably BitBit
08 — Landscape

Competitive Landscape

How comparable products position themselves visually, and where the gaps are.

Brand Identity Comparison

Brand Visual Metaphor Character? Lesson for BitBit
Claude (Anthropic) Radiating starburst (ideas outward) No character. Warm identity. Warm palette works for trust. Rust-orange on cream is their territory — we differentiate with dark bg.
ChatGPT (OpenAI) "Blossom" — growth, organic precision No character. Geometric logo. Rebrand focused on feeling more human. The blossom reads "organic math." Clinical.
Gemini (Google) Sparkle — guiding light Animation IS the character. Motion-first identity. The sparkle has states. Best "entity through animation" example.
Duolingo (Duo) Green owl with opinions Full character. Cultural icon. A mascot with opinions is more memorable than a friendly one. Duo has moods — guilt, pride, chaos.
Cleo (Fintech) "Big Sis Energy" Personality is the character (no visual mascot). $150M ARR. Entity branding drives revenue. Cleo has boundaries — pushes back on users.
Intercom Fin Converging arrows star Configurable identity per brand. Professional but no personality. "Fin Identities" let businesses customize — smart for B2B.
Discord Wumpus (community) + Clyde (bot) Dual mascot system. Wumpus in empty states turns dead moments into character moments. Two mascots = confusion.
Market Gap

Dark + warm orange + entity character is unoccupied territory. Linear owns dark+purple (professional-cold). Vercel owns dark+B&W (authority-cold). Claude owns warm-orange on light backgrounds. Nobody is doing dark background + warm accent + characterful entity. That's our space.

Key Takeaways from Research

  • Animation is the new logo. Gemini's sparkle is more recognizable in motion than as a static mark. For entity-first products, the animation system IS the brand identity.
  • Personality requires maintenance. A living character is an ongoing editorial commitment, not a one-time design deliverable.
  • The Tamagotchi pattern is underused in SaaS. Temporal awareness (notices your absence, remembers yesterday) is the cheapest way to make something feel alive.
  • Let the entity have limits. Clippy failed because it was always eager. Cleo succeeds because it pushes back. Boundaries = more real.
  • The Clippy lesson: A character without intelligence behind it is worse than no character at all. BitBit has the intelligence — we just need the face.

11 — Discussion

Questions for the Consultation

Prioritized questions to guide the conversation. Start with the strategic ones, drill into specifics as the discussion develops.

Strategic Direction

Given our positioning as an entity rather than a tool, what's the right level of visual embodiment?

We want something between Gemini's abstract sparkle and Duolingo's full character. Professional enough for B2B but warm enough for a tradie messaging from a job site.

How do we make BitBit feel alive across the entire product without it becoming distracting or gimmicky?

This is a productivity tool for busy people. The "entity" feel should enhance, not interrupt. Idle breathing animation in a chat avatar is fine. Random pop-ups are not.

Should our marketing and product share the same visual language, or is the intentional split appropriate?

Currently: cream + serif for landing, dark + sans for dashboard. Some brands do this deliberately (warm welcome vs. focused workspace). Others unify everything.

Character & Identity

Our existing logo has a floating detached corner piece that animates independently. Is there something here worth developing into a character element?

The corner piece already has: float, rotate, and scale animations. It behaves like a small companion to the main form. Could this evolve into BitBit's "face" or personality indicator?

What visual signals separate "smart entity" from "AI chatbot" in a user's mind?

Our target users include people who would dismiss "AI software." The character/identity needs to abstract away the AI and foreground the capability. "This works for me" not "this is AI."

How many expression states does a character need to feel alive without being over-designed?

Minimum: idle, thinking, working, done, error. But should there be: greeting, celebrating, apologizing, waiting, sleeping? Where's the diminishing return?

Visual System

Is our orange (#FF5A1F) the right primary, or should it be refined? It's vibrant. Some contexts want it softer.

Claude uses a muted rust (#C15F3C). Our orange is more energetic. Does energetic fit "capable entity" or should we pull toward warmer, more grounded tones?

What illustration style pairs well with our dark glassmorphic UI?

Need illustrations for: onboarding screens, empty states, error pages, marketing. The glassmorphism creates depth. Flat illustrations might clash. Gradient/glow might work.

Should we develop a custom icon set, or is a curated Lucide subset sufficient?

Custom icons are expensive to maintain but create brand distinction. A middle ground might be custom icons for core concepts (connections, agents, triage) and Lucide for standard UI.

What's the right approach to micro-interactions and celebration moments?

We have confetti, check-draws, and glow pulses. How far should we push the "delight" axis? Task completion, first connection, milestones — what earns a celebration?

Practical

What's the most impactful deliverable we should prioritize from this engagement?

Options: brand identity package, character design, UI component polish, illustration set, marketing redesign. We can't do everything at once. What moves the needle most?

What format should the brand guidelines take to be most useful for an AI-assisted development workflow?

Our development is heavily AI-assisted (Claude Code). Brand guidelines need to be machine-readable and concise enough to fit in an AI context window. Figma tokens? CSS variables? Markdown document?
